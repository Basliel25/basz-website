The first time someone asks you how expensive a context switch is, you'll look it up, and you'll find a number. Two microseconds. Five microseconds. It depends on your kernel. It depends on your CPU. It depends on whether _vdso_ is in the mood today. You'll write this number down somewhere, and you'll feel as though you have answered the question.

You have not answered the question.

The direct cost of a context switch — the cycles the kernel spends saving registers, flipping page tables, and reinstalling the timer — is usually small. It's the indirect cost that matters. When the scheduler puts your thread to sleep and wakes another one, you lose more than a few registers. You lose the L1 you'd warmed. You lose the branch history you'd trained. You lose, if you're unlucky, a TLB that took thirty pages of access patterns to populate. None of this shows up in `perf stat -e context-switches`. All of it shows up in the wall clock.

## Measuring what isn't there

The trick, when a cost is diffuse, is to build two worlds that differ only in the thing you're trying to measure, and then race them. Here's the experiment I kept coming back to: a tight loop that walks a 32MB hash table in one world, and the same loop interrupted every millisecond by a thread doing unrelated work in the other. Same hash table. Same loop. Same core. Different neighbours.

> "A model should be as simple as possible, and no simpler." I keep this taped above my monitor; it keeps me honest about the limits of the back of my napkin.

That last point matters. A context switch of the kind I'm describing rarely moves the median. It moves the 99th percentile. If you're mean-watching, you'll miss it and think you're fine. You are not fine. You are just looking at the wrong part of the curve.

## So what do we do about it?

Three things, in decreasing order of how cheaply you can do them:

1. **Measure the tail.** Every latency dashboard should show p99 and p99.9. If it shows only the mean, it is lying to you with numbers.
2. **Pin your hot threads.** If a thread is worth warming a cache for, it is worth pinning to a core. `sched_setaffinity` is three lines.
3. **Reduce the working set.** Smaller working sets pay a smaller indirect cost. This is the whole argument for cache-aware data structures, phrased in one sentence.

None of this is novel. All of it is the kind of advice that becomes visible only after you've gone looking for a cost you couldn't see.
